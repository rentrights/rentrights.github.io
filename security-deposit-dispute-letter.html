Security Deposit Dispute Letter — Free Templates & Complete Guide

Your landlord is keeping your deposit unfairly. Here are ready-to-send legal demand letters, step-by-step instructions, and everything you need to get your money back — without a lawyer.

Updated: July 2026 Read time: 12 minutes Includes: 3 Free Letter Templates
⚡ Quick Answer

A formal written demand letter is your most powerful first step to recover an unfairly withheld deposit. Most landlords return deposits after receiving a proper demand letter — because ignoring it means facing court with penalties of 2–3x the deposit amount in most states. Use our free templates below.

Before You Write — Gather These First

A strong demand letter is backed by evidence. Before writing, gather:

Your lease — confirms deposit amount and terms · Move-in photos — proves pre-existing conditions · Move-out photos — shows how you left the unit · Forwarding address proof — shows you gave landlord your address · Your state's deadline — check our Security Deposit Laws by State guide · Any itemization your landlord sent — if they sent deductions, you can dispute specific items.

⚠️ Send It Right

Send your demand letter via email AND certified mail with return receipt requested. Email creates a timestamp; certified mail creates legal proof of delivery. Keep copies of everything.

Taking It to Small Claims Court

If your landlord ignores both demand letters, small claims court is your next step. The good news: it is designed for people without lawyers, and security deposit cases are among the most common and straightforward cases heard there.

1

Find Your Local Small Claims Court

Search "[your county] small claims court." Filing fees are typically $30–$100. Most states allow claims up to $5,000–$10,000 in small claims court.

2

File Your Claim

Fill out the small claims complaint form. List your damages: the deposit amount plus the statutory penalty (2x or 3x depending on your state). Many courts now allow online filing.

3

Serve Your Landlord

The court will instruct you on how to serve the defendant. This is usually done by certified mail or through the court's process server.

4

Prepare Your Evidence

Print your move-in and move-out photos, lease, demand letters, and any receipts. Organize them chronologically. Bring 3 copies: one for you, one for the judge, one for your landlord.

5

Attend Your Hearing

Be calm, factual, and brief. Show the judge your evidence in order. Most security deposit hearings last 15–20 minutes. Judges in these cases are very familiar with tenant rights.

✅ You Will Likely Win If...

You have move-in photos showing the unit's prior condition · Your landlord missed the return deadline · Your landlord cannot produce receipts for claimed repairs · You left the unit in normal condition beyond expected wear and tear · Your landlord did not do a move-in inspection or have you sign a checklist.

Lease Termination Letter — Free Templates for Every Situation

What Is a Lease Termination Letter — And Why Does It Matter?

A lease termination letter (also called a notice to vacate) is a formal written notice from a tenant to their landlord stating their intention to end their tenancy and move out by a specific date. It is one of the most important documents in the rental process — and getting it wrong can cost you your security deposit or expose you to liability for additional months of rent.

Even if you and your landlord have a friendly relationship and you've spoken verbally about moving out, you still need a written notice. Verbal notices are hard to prove and do not satisfy legal requirements in any US state.

⚠️ Most Common Mistake

Tenants who forget to give proper written notice — or give it too late — often lose part of their security deposit or are held liable for additional rent. Check your lease for the required notice period BEFORE setting your move-out date.

The amount of notice you must give depends on your lease type and state law. Here are the most common requirements:

✅ Fixed Lease Rule

If you have a fixed-term lease (e.g., 12 months ending August 31), you typically do NOT need to give notice — the lease ends automatically. However, many leases require 30–60 days notice even for fixed leases if you plan NOT to renew. Always check your specific lease language.

How to Send Your Letter the Right Way

Always send two ways:

1. Email — Creates an automatic timestamp proving when you sent it. Keep the sent email in your records permanently.

2. Certified mail with return receipt — Creates a legal proof of delivery that is admissible in court. Keep the green return receipt card permanently.

Never rely on verbal notice alone. Never rely on text message alone. Never just slide a paper under the landlord's door.

✅ What to Keep Forever

Keep copies of: your termination letter, email sent confirmation, certified mail receipt, landlord's acknowledgment (if received), and your lease. Even after you've moved out and gotten your deposit back, keep these for at least 3 years — the statute of limitations for contract disputes in most states.

After You Send Your Letter

What happens next: Your landlord should acknowledge receipt. Begin your move-out preparation immediately — use our Move-Out Checklist to maximize your deposit return. Know your state's deposit return deadline and mark it on your calendar.

If your landlord tries to hold you to the lease after proper notice: They generally cannot do so if your notice was properly given and legally sufficient. If they attempt to charge additional rent beyond your notice period, send them a written response citing your state's notice requirements and keep all documentation.

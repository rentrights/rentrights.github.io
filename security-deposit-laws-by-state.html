Updated June 2026  ·  10 min read  ·  All 50 States Covered
⚡ Quick Answer

What Is a Security Deposit?

A security deposit is money paid by a tenant to a landlord before moving in. It is held by the landlord as financial protection against unpaid rent, property damage beyond normal wear and tear, or other lease violations.

Critically — a security deposit is not a fee. It is your money. The landlord is holding it on your behalf, and must return it (minus any lawful deductions) when your tenancy ends.

How Much Can a Landlord Charge?

Many states cap the maximum security deposit a landlord can collect. The most common limits are 1 month's rent or 2 months' rent. Some states have no cap at all.

✅ Tip

If your landlord charged a deposit above your state's legal limit, you may be able to recover the excess amount — and in some states, additional penalties. Always check your state's specific rule below.

What Can a Landlord Legally Deduct?

Landlords can deduct from your deposit for:

  • Unpaid rent or utility bills you owe
  • Damage to the property beyond normal wear and tear
  • Cleaning costs if you left the unit significantly dirtier than you found it
  • Broken appliances, fixtures, or furniture caused by the tenant
  • Costs to replace items you removed or damaged

Landlords cannot deduct for:

  • Normal wear and tear (small nail holes, faded paint, worn carpet)
  • Repairs that were already needed before you moved in
  • General cleaning if you left the unit reasonably clean
  • Repainting simply because time has passed
  • Damage caused by the landlord or their contractors

How to Get Your Security Deposit Back

Follow these steps to maximize your chances of getting your full deposit returned:

1

Do a Move-In Inspection

Before or on day one, photograph every room thoroughly. Document any existing damage and have your landlord sign a move-in checklist. This is your most powerful protection.

2

Give Proper Written Notice Before Moving Out

Most leases require 30–60 days written notice before you vacate. Failing to do this can cost you part of your deposit.

3

Clean Thoroughly and Repair Minor Damage

Return the unit in the same condition you found it. Fill small nail holes, clean appliances, and remove all belongings. The cost of cleaning crews comes out of your deposit.

4

Do a Move-Out Walkthrough

Ask your landlord to do a final walkthrough with you present. Take photos of the unit when you leave. Get your landlord's signature if possible.

5

Provide Your Forwarding Address in Writing

Your landlord needs to know where to send your deposit. Send this in writing — email is fine — so you have a record.

What If Your Landlord Keeps Your Deposit Illegally?

If your landlord misses the return deadline or makes improper deductions, you have strong legal options in most states:

Your Options

1. Send a demand letter — Write a formal letter citing your state's law and demanding return within 7–14 days.

2. File in small claims court — Most security deposit disputes are resolved here. No lawyer needed. Filing fees are usually $30–$100.

3. Claim penalties — Many states award 2–3x the wrongfully withheld amount plus attorney fees.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can my landlord use my security deposit for last month's rent?
Only if your lease specifically allows this. In many states, using a deposit for rent without agreement is a violation.

Does my landlord have to pay interest on my security deposit?
In some states (Massachusetts, New Jersey, Illinois, and others), yes. Check your state's specific rules above.

What if my landlord never did a move-in inspection?
This actually helps you — in many states, the landlord's failure to document pre-existing damage means they cannot legally charge you for it.

Can my landlord deduct for carpet cleaning?
Only if the carpet is significantly dirtier than normal. Carpets wear out over time — routine cleaning after a long tenancy is often considered normal wear and tear.

My landlord sent a list of deductions but they seem made up. What do I do?
Request receipts for all claimed repairs. In most states, landlords must provide itemized receipts. If they can't, those deductions may be invalid.

How to Handle Illegal Security Deposit Deductions in California (2026)

Your landlord kept part of your deposit — but are those deductions actually legal? California has some of the country's strongest deposit protection laws, and unlawful deductions can cost your landlord twice what they wrongfully took.

Updated: July 2026 Read time: 14 minutes Authority: CA Civil Code § 1950.5 Reviewed by: RentRights Legal Team
⚡ Featured Answer — Illegal Security Deposit Deductions in California

In California, a security deposit deduction is illegal if it charges for: normal wear and tear, pre-existing damage, cleaning beyond what is reasonably necessary, repairs the landlord was already obligated to make, or costs without itemized receipts. Under California Civil Code § 1950.5, landlords who make unlawful deductions can be sued for the wrongfully withheld amount plus up to 2x that amount in punitive damages, plus attorney fees. You must act within the statute of limitations — generally 2 years for most deposit claims.

  • Landlord has 21 calendar days to return deposit or provide written itemization
  • Receipts required for all deductions over $125
  • Penalty for bad faith withholding: deposit + 2x deposit + attorney fees
  • Small claims court handles claims up to $12,500

Introduction

You moved out. You left the apartment clean. You took photos. You provided your forwarding address. And then — weeks later — your landlord returns only a fraction of your deposit, with a vague list of deductions that don't add up.

This scenario plays out thousands of times every year across California. Many tenants assume they have no choice but to accept whatever their landlord decides. That assumption is wrong.

California has one of the most tenant-friendly security deposit laws in the country — California Civil Code § 1950.5. It sets strict rules on what landlords can deduct, how they must document those deductions, and when they must return the deposit. Violations come with serious financial consequences for landlords.

This guide walks you through exactly what constitutes an illegal deduction in California, how to build your case, how to write a demand letter that gets results, and how to take your landlord to small claims court if necessary.

What Is an Illegal Security Deposit Deduction in California?

Not every deduction a landlord makes is legal. Under California Civil Code § 1950.5(b), a landlord may only use a security deposit for four specific purposes:

  1. Unpaid rent
  2. Cleaning the unit — but only to the level of cleanliness that existed when the tenant moved in
  3. Repairing damage caused by the tenant beyond normal wear and tear
  4. Replacing or restoring personal property furnished by the landlord (if permitted by the lease)

Any deduction that falls outside these four categories is legally unauthorized. This includes charging for things the landlord was already obligated to do, cosmetic upgrades, or damage that existed before you moved in.

⚠️ The Single Most Important Rule

California landlords cannot charge tenants for normal wear and tear — under any circumstances, for any reason. This is explicitly stated in Civil Code § 1950.5(b)(2) and is one of the most commonly violated rules in California landlord-tenant law.

California Security Deposit Law — Civil Code § 1950.5

California Civil Code § 1950.5 is the primary law governing security deposits in the state. Here are the key provisions every tenant should know:

Maximum Deposit Amount

As of April 1, 2025, California law limits security deposits to one month's rent for unfurnished units (SB 611). This applies to most residential tenancies. Prior to this change, the limit was two months' rent for unfurnished units.

If your landlord collected a deposit larger than one month's rent after April 2025, the excess amount may be recoverable.

The 21-Day Requirement

After you vacate, your landlord has exactly 21 calendar days to do one of two things:

  • Return your full deposit, OR
  • Send you an itemized written statement of deductions, with copies of receipts for all repairs and cleaning costs exceeding $125

The clock starts on the day you actually vacate — not the last day of your lease. If you hand over keys on August 28, the deadline is September 18.

Receipt Requirements

Under California law, if any deduction is made for cleaning or repairs, the landlord must provide:

  • An itemized written statement listing each deduction
  • The amount of each deduction
  • Copies of invoices, receipts, or bills for work that cost more than $125

A landlord who simply writes "cleaning: $400" without providing a receipt has not complied with the law. That deduction is contestable.

The 2025 Photo Documentation Requirement (SB 611)

Effective April 1, 2025, California landlords must provide tenants with photographs of the rental unit at both move-in and move-out. These photos must be provided before any deductions are made from the deposit. A landlord who fails to provide move-in photos loses significant legal standing in any deposit dispute.

Legal Time Limits — Don't Miss These Deadlines

21d
Day 21 after move-out: Landlord must return deposit or mail itemized statement with receipts. This is the most critical deadline in California deposit law.
14d
After receiving itemization: Send your written dispute to the landlord within 14 days of receiving their deduction statement for strongest legal position (not legally required but strategically important).
2yr
Statute of limitations: You generally have 2 years from the date you should have received your deposit to file a small claims lawsuit. Don't wait — evidence disappears and memories fade.
🚨 If Landlord Misses the 21-Day Deadline

A landlord who fails to provide the itemized statement within 21 days may forfeit their right to make any deductions at all — even for legitimate damage. California courts have consistently held that strict compliance with the 21-day deadline is required. Late itemizations can be challenged as legally invalid.

Common Illegal Charges in California

These are the deductions California landlords most commonly attempt — and which are most commonly challenged successfully in small claims court:

1. Normal Wear and Tear

This is the #1 source of illegal deductions in California. Wear and tear is the gradual, inevitable deterioration that results from ordinary, reasonable use of a property over time. California law is explicit: landlords cannot charge for it.

Examples of wear and tear that cannot be charged:

  • Small nail holes from hanging pictures
  • Faded, slightly scuffed paint after a normal tenancy
  • Carpet worn thin from regular foot traffic
  • Minor scuffs and marks on hardwood floors
  • Worn door handles, hinges, or locks from regular use
  • Hard water spots on faucets or glass

2. Repainting the Entire Unit

One of the most disputed issues in California deposit cases. California courts have consistently held that repainting walls after a normal tenancy — especially a long one — is ordinary wear and tear. A landlord who charges $1,500 to repaint an apartment the tenant lived in for three years is almost certainly making an illegal deduction.

The exception: if you painted walls an unauthorized color, made large holes, or caused significant staining, repainting that specific area may be chargeable.

3. Carpet Replacement After Normal Wear

California has actually issued guidance on carpet: carpet is generally considered to have a useful life of approximately 8-10 years. If the carpet was old when you moved in and simply worn from normal use, the landlord cannot charge for full replacement. At most, they can charge for the remaining useful life — and that prorated amount is often very small.

4. Professional Cleaning Beyond Move-In Condition

Landlords can only charge for cleaning to restore the unit to the condition it was in when you moved in — not to a higher standard. If you moved into an apartment that was not professionally cleaned, you cannot be charged for professional cleaning when you leave.

This is why your move-in documentation matters so much. If you have photos showing the unit was not perfectly clean at move-in, you have direct evidence against excessive cleaning charges.

5. Pre-Existing Damage

Any damage that existed before you moved in cannot be charged to you. This is why California's new SB 611 photo requirement is so significant — move-in photos create an undeniable baseline of the unit's condition before your tenancy.

6. Deductions Without Receipts (Over $125)

If a landlord deducts for repairs or cleaning that cost over $125 but doesn't provide itemized receipts, those deductions violate California Civil Code § 1950.5(g). Unsupported deductions can be challenged and are frequently overturned in small claims court.

7. Inflated or Unreasonable Repair Costs

Even with receipts, repair charges must be reasonable. If a landlord charges $800 to replace a light fixture that costs $40 at a hardware store, that charge is subject to challenge. California courts look at whether the costs were reasonable and necessary.

How to File in California Small Claims Court

If your demand letter is ignored, California Small Claims Court is your next step. The process is designed for individuals without attorneys, and security deposit cases are among the most common — and most tenant-friendly — cases heard there.

1

Find Your Courthouse

File in the county where the rental property was located — not where you currently live. Search "[county name] small claims court California" to find the courthouse and filing procedures. Many California courts now offer online filing.

2

Calculate Your Claim Amount

Your total claim can include: the wrongfully withheld deposit amount + up to 2x that amount in punitive damages (if bad faith is found) + any actual damages you suffered (e.g., cost of replacement items). California small claims maximum is $12,500.

3

File Form SC-100

Complete California Judicial Council Form SC-100 (Plaintiff's Claim and ORDER to Go to Small Claims Court). Filing fees range from $30 to $75 depending on claim amount. Many California courts allow filing online at courts.ca.gov.

4

Serve the Defendant

You must properly serve your landlord with notice of the lawsuit. California law allows service by certified mail (easiest), process server, or sheriff. The court can instruct you on approved methods.

5

Organize Your Evidence

Print all photos, emails, your lease, demand letter, and the landlord's itemization. Organize everything chronologically. Bring 3 copies: one for you, one for the judge, one for your landlord. Write a brief timeline of key events.

6

Attend Your Hearing

Arrive early. Dress professionally. When the judge addresses you, state your case factually and briefly. Present your evidence in order. Emphasize: (1) what the deposit was, (2) what you got back, (3) why the deductions are illegal under Civil Code § 1950.5, and (4) your photographic evidence.

✅ What Judges Look For

California small claims judges in deposit cases specifically examine: Was there a move-in inspection with documentation? Do move-in and move-out photos exist? Are the claimed deductions within the four permitted categories? Were receipts provided? Was the 21-day deadline met? Strong answers to these questions win cases.

What California Landlords Absolutely Cannot Charge For

  • Normal wear and tear — explicitly prohibited by Civil Code § 1950.5(b)(2)
  • Pre-existing damage — damage that existed before the tenant moved in
  • Cleaning above move-in condition — can only restore, not upgrade
  • Cosmetic upgrades — new paint color, updated fixtures that are improvements, not restorations
  • General maintenance — replacing furnace filters, caulking tubs, routine upkeep
  • Items past their useful life — replacing 15-year-old carpet solely due to age
  • Costs without receipts (over $125) — documentation is required by law
  • Your share of repairs the landlord needed to make anyway — pre-existing maintenance obligations
  • Landscaping or exterior maintenance — unless explicitly in your lease and caused by you
  • Future rent — the deposit can only cover rent that is currently owed, not speculative future losses

Exceptions — When Some Deductions Are Legal

Not every deduction is illegal. Understanding the legitimate exceptions helps you evaluate your landlord's itemization clearly:

1. Damage Beyond Normal Wear and Tear

Large holes in walls, burns on carpet, broken fixtures from misuse, deep gouges in hardwood floors, unauthorized modifications — these are legitimate damage that landlords can charge for. The key question is always whether a reasonable tenant would have caused this damage through normal use.

2. Extraordinary Cleaning

If you left the unit significantly dirtier than you found it — grease-covered ovens, mold-covered bathrooms, garbage left behind — cleaning charges that restore the unit to its move-in condition are legitimate. The standard is the condition at move-in, not "like new."

3. Unpaid Rent

Rent that you legally owe and haven't paid can be deducted from your security deposit. However, if you believe you don't owe the rent being claimed — for example, because you provided proper notice but the landlord claims you owe additional months — dispute that separately in writing.

4. Contractual Items

If your lease specifically stated that certain items (like carpet cleaning) would be deducted regardless of condition, those contractual terms may be enforceable — unless they conflict with California law. Some carpet cleaning clauses have been found enforceable; others have been struck down. This is an area where consulting a California attorney can be valuable.

Key Takeaways — California Illegal Deposit Deductions

  • California Civil Code § 1950.5 strictly limits what landlords can deduct from your security deposit to four specific categories
  • Landlords have exactly 21 calendar days after move-out to return the deposit or provide a written itemization with receipts
  • Normal wear and tear is explicitly prohibited from deduction under California law — no exceptions
  • Receipts are required for any deduction involving repairs or cleaning over $125 — without them, the deduction is legally challengeable
  • As of April 2025, California landlords must provide move-in AND move-out photos before making any deposit deductions
  • Bad faith withholding can result in double damages plus attorney fees — making illegal deductions very costly for landlords
  • A well-written demand letter resolves many disputes before court — always send before filing
  • California Small Claims Court handles deposit disputes up to $12,500 — no attorney needed
  • The statute of limitations is generally 2 years from when the deposit should have been returned
  • Documentation is everything: move-in photos, move-out photos, and written communications are your most powerful tools

Conclusion

California's security deposit laws are among the strongest in the country — but only if you know how to use them. Every tenant deserves to have their deposit returned promptly and with only lawful deductions. Every deduction should be supported by documentation, limited to the four authorized purposes, and provided within the strict 21-day deadline.

If your landlord has made deductions you believe are unlawful, you have real options. Start with a formal demand letter citing California Civil Code § 1950.5. Most disputes resolve at this stage. If your landlord ignores your letter, California Small Claims Court provides an accessible, attorney-free path to recovering your money — plus potential double damages and attorney fees.

The most important thing you can do starting today, regardless of where you are in the rental cycle: document everything. Photos, emails, receipts, checklists — these documents are worth far more than any cleanup effort when it comes time to get your deposit back.

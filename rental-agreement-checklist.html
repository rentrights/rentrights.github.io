What Should You Check Before Signing a Rental Agreement?

§1

Why Reading a Rental Agreement Carefully Matters

A rental agreement isn't just paperwork — it's the rulebook for your entire tenancy.

It decides how much you pay and when, what happens if something breaks, whether you can have a roommate or pet, how you can end the lease, and what you lose if you break a rule.

Landlords write these agreements to protect their property and income, which is fair. But it also means the terms often favor the landlord unless you negotiate or catch a problem early.

Warning: Once you sign, verbal promises from the landlord — like "I'll fix that before you move in" — usually don't count unless they're written into the lease.

Taking thirty extra minutes to review a lease can save you hundreds, even thousands, of dollars later.

§2

Rent and Payment Terms

Start with the basics. Confirm these details match what you agreed to verbally or saw in the listing.

  • Exact monthly rent amount
  • Due date — the 1st? The 5th?
  • Accepted payment methods: check, online portal, money order
  • Late fee amount and grace period
  • Whether rent can increase during the lease term
  • Notice period required before a rent increase, for month-to-month leases
Real-life example

Maria signed a lease listing rent at $1,450 per month. She later found a separate clause charging a $75 "processing fee" for online payments — effectively raising her rent. Reading the payment section closely would have caught this before signing.

Warning: Some leases allow rent increases mid-term if certain conditions are met. Confirm your rent is locked for the full lease term unless you agree otherwise.

§3

Security Deposit Details

Security deposits are one of the most common sources of landlord-tenant disputes in the country.

  • Deposit amount
  • Whether it's refundable, and under what conditions
  • Timeline for return after move-out
  • Allowed deductions: damage, unpaid rent, cleaning
  • Whether the deposit is held in a separate account — required in some states
  • Non-refundable fees disguised as "deposits"

§4

Lease Duration and Renewal Terms

Know exactly how long you're committing to stay, and what happens when that period ends.

  • Start and end date of the lease
  • Whether it automatically converts to month-to-month
  • Renewal notice requirements: 30, 60, or 90 days
  • Whether rent changes upon renewal

Fixed-term vs. month-to-month

Fixed-term lease: locks you in for a set period, usually twelve months. More stability, but harder to leave early.

Month-to-month lease: more flexibility, but landlords can often end it with shorter notice than a fixed lease.

§5

Maintenance and Repair Responsibilities

This section decides who fixes what, and who pays for it.

  • Landlord's obligation to maintain habitable conditions
  • Tenant's responsibility for minor repairs — light bulbs, filters
  • How to report repair issues: in writing, by app, and so on
  • Expected response time for urgent repairs, like heating or plumbing
  • Whether you can hire your own repair service and deduct the cost
Real-life example

James rented an apartment where the lease stated tenants were responsible for "all appliance repairs." When the refrigerator broke, he had to pay for it himself. Checking this clause beforehand could have let him negotiate it out or budget for it.

§6

Rules on Subletting and Guests

Many leases restrict who can live in, or stay at, the property.

  • Whether subletting is allowed, and under what process
  • Guest stay limits — some leases cap guests at 14 days per year
  • Rules for adding a roommate mid-lease
  • Consequences for violating occupancy limits

Warning: Violating subletting rules, even unintentionally, can be grounds for lease termination in many agreements.

§7

Pet Policies

If you have pets, or plan to get one, this section is critical.

  • Whether pets are allowed at all
  • Breed or weight restrictions
  • Pet deposit or monthly pet rent
  • Number of pets allowed
  • Rules for service animals and emotional support animals — these often carry different legal protections than regular pets

Note: Federal fair housing rules provide specific protections for service animals and emotional support animals, separate from standard "no pets" policies. Rules can vary by state and locality, so confirm details if this applies to you.

§8

Utilities and Additional Fees

Rent isn't always the only cost. Hidden fees can add up fast.

  • Which utilities are included: water, gas, trash, internet
  • Estimated monthly utility costs, if not included
  • Parking fees
  • Amenity fees: gym, pool, storage
  • Application or administrative fees
  • Renter's insurance requirements

§9

Early Termination and Break Clauses

Life changes. Job relocations, family emergencies, or financial shifts can force you to move before the lease ends.

  • Whether an early termination clause exists
  • Penalty fees for breaking the lease early
  • Required notice period
  • Military clause — protections for active-duty service members
  • Whether you're allowed to find a replacement tenant

Warning: Without an early termination clause, you may owe rent through the entire lease term, even if you move out early.

§10

Property Condition and Move-In Inspection

Before you sign, and definitely before you move in, document the property's condition.

  • A move-in inspection checklist or form
  • Existing damage noted in writing, with photos
  • Working smoke detectors and carbon monoxide detectors
  • Functioning locks and windows
  • Signs of pests, mold, or water damage
Real-life example

Priya moved into an apartment with a small stain on the carpet that was already there. She didn't document it. At move-out, the landlord charged her for carpet replacement — and with no proof the stain pre-dated her lease, she had no way to dispute the charge.

Tip: Take timestamped photos of every room, closet, and appliance before you move in. Email them to your landlord or property manager as a record.

§11

State-Specific Rental Laws to Know

Rental laws differ significantly across the United States. Areas that commonly vary by state include:

  • Security deposit limits and return timelines
  • Notice periods for rent increases
  • Habitability standards
  • Eviction procedures
  • Rent control, applicable in some cities, not all states
  • Rules on withholding rent for repairs

Because these laws vary widely, always check your specific state and local housing authority before signing a lease or acting on assumptions about your rights. For complex situations, a local tenant rights organization or licensed attorney can review the lease before you sign.

§12

Common Mistakes Renters Make

  • Not reading the entire document. Skimming leads to missed fees and restrictive clauses.
  • Trusting verbal promises. If it's not written down, it may not be enforceable.
  • Skipping the move-in inspection. This creates disputes over damage later.
  • Ignoring the fine print on fees. Application, late, and "convenience" fees add up.
  • Not asking about repair response times. Vague language can leave you without heat for days.
  • Overlooking pet or guest policies. Assumptions lead to lease violations.
  • Not keeping a signed copy. Always request a fully executed copy for your records.
  • Overlooking renewal and termination terms. These affect your long-term flexibility.

§14

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the most important thing to check before signing a rental agreement?

Rent amount, security deposit terms, and lease duration are the top three priorities, since they carry the biggest financial impact.

Can a landlord change lease terms after I sign?

Generally, no, not without your written consent, unless the lease specifically allows changes such as rent increases on month-to-month agreements with proper notice.

Is a verbal agreement with a landlord legally binding?

Verbal agreements can be harder to enforce. It's safer to get every promise in writing within the lease itself.

What should I do if I find an unfair clause in the lease?

You can negotiate it with the landlord before signing, ask for it to be removed, or consult a tenant rights organization for guidance.

Do I need renter's insurance?

Some leases require it. Even if not required, it's generally a good idea to protect your personal belongings.

What happens if I break my lease early?

This depends on your lease terms and state law. You may owe remaining rent, a penalty fee, or be able to find a replacement tenant, depending on your agreement.

How do I know if my security deposit is protected?

Ask whether it's held in a separate account and request written documentation. Rules on this vary by state.

Can I negotiate the terms of a rental agreement?

Yes, many terms are negotiable before signing, including rent, pet fees, and move-in date. Once signed, changes typically require mutual written agreement.

What should I photograph before moving in?

Every room, existing damage, appliances, flooring, and fixtures. Timestamped photos protect you during move-out disputes.

Are month-to-month leases riskier than fixed-term leases?

They offer more flexibility but often allow landlords to end the tenancy with shorter notice, so weigh your need for stability against your need for flexibility.

Should I have a lawyer review my lease?

For complex leases, commercial rentals, or specific concerns about a clause, having an attorney or tenant rights organization review it can be a smart investment.

California Tenant Rights — Complete 2026 Guide

California has some of the strongest renter protections in the United States — from statewide rent caps to just-cause eviction requirements. Here is everything California renters need to know in 2026.

Updated: July 2026 Read time: 13 minutes Coverage: California Only
⚡ Quick Answer

California renters are protected by: a 5% + CPI rent cap (AB 1482), just-cause eviction requirements, a 21-day deposit return deadline with 2x penalties, 24-hour entry notice, and some of the strongest anti-harassment and retaliation laws in the country. Many cities add even stronger local protections.

AB 1482 — California's Statewide Rent Control Law

California's Tenant Protection Act of 2019 (AB 1482) established statewide rent control — the first in California history. It applies to most residential rental units across the state, with important exemptions.

AB 1482 — Tenant Protection Act

Annual Rent Increase Limit: 5% + Local CPI (Max 10%)

Landlords covered by AB 1482 may only increase rent once per year by the lower of: 5% plus the regional Consumer Price Index (CPI), or 10% total. In most California regions in 2026, CPI is running below 5%, making the effective cap below 10%. Any increase above this limit is illegal and can be challenged.

What AB 1482 Covers

AB 1482 applies to most residential rentals in California, including apartments, condos, and single-family homes rented by corporate landlords. Coverage requires the unit to be at least 15 years old (built before July 2011 for 2026 purposes).

What AB 1482 Does NOT Cover

The following are exempt from AB 1482: single-family homes rented by individual owners (with proper exemption notice given to tenant) · condos sold separately (with proper notice) · buildings built within the last 15 years · subsidized housing with independent rent restrictions · duplexes where the owner lives in one unit · dormitories.

⚠️ Check Your Notice

If you live in a single-family home or condo, your landlord must have given you written notice that the unit is exempt from AB 1482. If you never received this notice, your unit may actually be covered even if the landlord claims otherwise.

Just-Cause Eviction — California 2026

AB 1482 also established just-cause eviction protections for covered units. After 12 months of tenancy, a landlord must have a legally recognized reason ("just cause") to evict you.

At-Fault Just Cause (Tenant Did Something)

Nonpayment of rent · Breach of a material lease term after written notice · Maintaining a nuisance · Criminal activity · Subletting without permission · Refusing landlord access after proper notice · Using the unit for an unlawful purpose.

No-Fault Just Cause (Tenant Did Nothing Wrong)

Owner or family member moving into the unit · Substantial remodel requiring tenant to vacate · Demolition of the unit · Withdrawal from the rental market (Ellis Act).

✅ Relocation Assistance Required

For ALL no-fault evictions under AB 1482, California law requires landlords to pay tenants one month's rent as relocation assistance. This payment must be made before or when the notice is served. Failure to pay relocation assistance may make the eviction illegal.

Landlord Entry Rights in California

California Civil Code § 1954 governs when and how a landlord may enter a rental unit:

24-hour notice required for: repairs or inspections · showing the unit to prospective tenants or buyers · delivering important notices · conducting pre-move-out inspections.

Emergency exception: No notice required for genuine emergencies only — fire, gas leak, burst pipe, or other immediate threat to health or safety.

Written notice is strongly preferred and may be required in some circumstances. Notice must specify the date, approximate time, and purpose of entry.

Reasonable hours only: Entry must occur between 8am and 8pm, or at another time agreed upon by the tenant.

Repair and Habitability Rights

California landlords must maintain rental units in a habitable condition under Civil Code § 1941. This includes: effective waterproofing and weather protection · working plumbing and water supply · adequate heating · electrical systems in good working order · clean and sanitary conditions · functional windows and doors · freedom from rodents and vermin.

Repair and Deduct — California

California allows tenants to use the repair-and-deduct remedy under Civil Code § 1942. After giving the landlord written notice and a reasonable time to repair (typically 30 days unless urgent), a tenant can hire a contractor and deduct up to one month's rent from the next rent payment. This remedy can only be used twice in any 12-month period.

Rent Withholding — California

California tenants may also withhold rent if a unit is substantially uninhabitable — but this is a complex legal remedy with strict requirements. Tenants who withhold rent incorrectly risk eviction. Consult a California tenant rights attorney before withholding rent.

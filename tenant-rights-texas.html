Texas Tenant Rights — Complete 2026 Guide

Texas is a landlord-friendly state — but tenants still have significant legal protections. From the strongest security deposit penalty law in the country to strict eviction procedures, here is everything Texas renters need to know in 2026.

Updated: July 2026 Read time: 12 minutes Coverage: All of Texas
⚡ Quick Answer

Texas tenants are protected by: a 30-day deposit return deadline with a 3x penalty (strongest in the US), strict eviction procedures requiring court action, the right to repair-and-deduct after proper notice, and protection against retaliatory eviction. Texas has no rent control, but the procedural protections are meaningful.

Texas Security Deposit Laws — Strongest Penalties in the US

Texas has one of the most tenant-favorable security deposit penalty laws in the entire country. While many states impose 2x damages for wrongful withholding, Texas imposes 3x the deposit amount plus $100 plus attorney fees for bad faith retention. This makes fighting for your deposit in Texas extremely worthwhile.

⚠️ Critical — Forwarding Address

In Texas, the 30-day clock does NOT start until you provide your forwarding address in writing. If you move out without giving a forwarding address, your landlord has no legal deadline to return the deposit. Always send your forwarding address via email AND certified mail on or before your move-out date.

What Texas Landlords Can and Cannot Deduct

Texas law allows deductions for: unpaid rent · damage beyond normal wear and tear · costs for which the tenant is legally responsible under the lease.

Texas landlords cannot deduct for: normal wear and tear · pre-existing damage · repairs the landlord was already obligated to make · anything not covered by the lease or state law.

The Texas Eviction Process — What Landlords Must Do

In Texas, a landlord cannot remove a tenant without going through the formal court process — no matter what. Self-help eviction (changing locks, removing belongings, shutting off utilities) is illegal in Texas and can result in significant liability for the landlord.

1

Written Notice to Vacate

For nonpayment of rent: 3-day notice (unless lease specifies more). For lease violations: reasonable notice to cure, then notice to vacate. For month-to-month tenants: 30-day notice. For at-will tenants: 3-day notice minimum.

2

File Eviction Suit (Forcible Entry & Detainer)

If tenant doesn't leave, landlord must file in the local Justice of the Peace court. Filing fee is typically $46–$100. A hearing is scheduled within 10–21 days.

3

Court Hearing

Both landlord and tenant can present their case. You have the right to attend and defend yourself. Bring your lease, payment records, and any communications with the landlord.

4

Judgment and Appeal

If the judge rules for the landlord, you have 5 days to appeal to County Court. Filing a bond or pauper's oath allows you to stay during appeal. This gives you additional time and another chance to present your case.

5

Writ of Possession

Only after all appeals are exhausted can a constable physically remove you. This process typically takes 3-6 weeks from initial notice — landlords cannot shortcut it.

✅ Your Eviction Defense Rights

At your Texas eviction hearing, you can raise defenses including: landlord failed to make required repairs · landlord accepted rent after giving notice (waiver) · eviction is retaliatory · notice was procedurally defective · landlord violated the lease first. Even in Texas, a well-prepared tenant can successfully defend against an eviction.

Repair Rights in Texas

Under Texas Property Code § 92.052, landlords must make repairs that materially affect the health or safety of an ordinary tenant. This includes: heating and air conditioning · plumbing · electrical systems · structural safety · pest control in certain circumstances.

The Texas Repair-and-Deduct Process

Texas has a specific repair-and-deduct procedure that tenants must follow carefully:

  • Send written notice of the needed repair to the landlord
  • Give the landlord a reasonable time to make repairs — courts typically interpret this as 7 days for urgent repairs, more for non-urgent ones
  • If landlord fails to repair, you may have the right to terminate the lease, deduct repair costs from rent (up to one month's rent), or both
  • You must be current on rent and not in violation of the lease to use this remedy
⚠️ Texas Repair Warning

The Texas repair-and-deduct remedy has strict requirements. Tenants who attempt it incorrectly can face eviction for nonpayment of rent. If the repair issue is serious, consult Texas Legal Aid or a tenant attorney before withholding rent or deducting repair costs.

Landlord Entry Rights in Texas

Texas law is less specific than California or New York on landlord entry notice requirements. Texas Property Code does not specify an exact notice period — instead requiring "reasonable notice" for most non-emergency entries.

In practice, Texas courts and tenant advocates apply a 24-hour notice standard as reasonable. Your lease may specify a longer notice period, which the landlord must honor.

Emergency entry (fire, gas leak, burst pipe, immediate health/safety threat) requires no advance notice.

Repeated entry without reasonable notice may constitute harassment and interference with quiet enjoyment — a lease violation that can support a claim for damages.

Retaliation Protections in Texas

Texas Property Code § 92.331 prohibits landlords from retaliating against tenants who: report housing code violations to a government agency · exercise a legal right · participate in a tenant organization.

If a landlord retaliates within 6 months of a protected activity, Texas law creates a presumption of retaliation — shifting the burden to the landlord to prove they had a legitimate non-retaliatory reason for their action.

Remedies for retaliation include: one month's rent plus $500 · actual damages · court costs and attorney fees · injunctive relief.

No Rent Control in Texas

Texas state law preempts all local rent control ordinances. No Texas city or county can legally limit rent increases. Landlords in Texas can raise rent by any amount with proper notice — 30 days for month-to-month tenancies, or at lease renewal for fixed-term leases.

During a fixed lease term, rent cannot increase at all — the lease amount is locked in for the term. Only at renewal can the landlord propose a new rent amount, which you can accept or decline.

💡 Texas Renters — What You Can Do About High Rents

While you cannot challenge a rent increase legally in Texas, you can: negotiate with your landlord (many will negotiate to avoid turnover costs) · organize with other tenants in your building · document habitability issues which may give you legal leverage · consult a Texas tenant attorney if an increase seems connected to protected activity (retaliation).

New York Tenant Rights — Complete 2026 Guide

New York has some of the most powerful tenant protections in the United States — from NYC's massive rent stabilization system to statewide just-cause eviction rules. Here is everything New York renters need to know in 2026.

Updated: July 2026 Read time: 13 minutes Coverage: New York State + NYC
⚡ Quick Answer

New York renters benefit from: NYC rent stabilization (2.75% increase cap for 2026), statewide just-cause eviction protections under the 2024 Good Cause Eviction Law, a 14-day deposit return deadline with 2x penalties, 24-hour entry notice, and the landmark Housing Stability and Tenant Protection Act (HSTPA) of 2019 which dramatically strengthened these rights.

Housing Stability and Tenant Protection Act (HSTPA) 2019

The Housing Stability and Tenant Protection Act of 2019 was the most significant overhaul of New York tenant law in decades. It fundamentally changed the balance of power between landlords and tenants across the state.

HSTPA 2019 — Key Changes

What the HSTPA Changed for New York Renters

The HSTPA eliminated most pathways landlords previously used to deregulate rent-stabilized apartments, capped security deposits at one month's rent statewide, strengthened harassment protections, extended notice periods for lease non-renewals, and gave tenants the right to sue landlords for violations in housing court. It remains the foundation of modern New York tenant law.

Before HSTPA, landlords could remove apartments from rent stabilization when rents exceeded a certain threshold (vacancy decontrol) or after a tenant earning above a certain income lived there. The HSTPA effectively ended both pathways, preserving the rent-stabilized status of approximately one million NYC apartments indefinitely.

⚠️ Key HSTPA Provisions Still in Effect

Security deposits capped at one month's rent statewide · Landlords must return deposits within 14 days · 30–90 day non-renewal notice required based on tenancy length · Harassment of tenants is a housing court violation · Tenants can recover attorney fees in successful harassment cases.

Good Cause Eviction Law — New York 2024

In April 2024, New York passed the Good Cause Eviction Law as part of the state budget — a major expansion of tenant protections statewide. This law applies outside of NYC's existing rent stabilization system and gives many more New York renters meaningful eviction protections for the first time.

What Good Cause Eviction Does

For covered tenants, landlords must now have a legally recognized "good cause" reason to either evict a tenant OR refuse to renew their lease. This is a massive change from the prior system, where landlords in most of New York State could simply refuse to renew a lease without any reason.

Covered tenants can only be evicted for:

  • Nonpayment of rent
  • Violation of a substantial lease obligation (after written notice and opportunity to cure)
  • Nuisance behavior affecting other tenants
  • Illegal use of the premises
  • Owner occupancy (landlord or immediate family moving in)
  • Demolition or substantial rehabilitation requiring vacancy

Rent Increase Protection Under Good Cause

The Good Cause law also limits rent increases. Landlords covered by the law can only raise rent by the lower of 5% or the local Consumer Price Index plus 5%. Increases above this threshold can be used as a defense against eviction for nonpayment.

Who Is Covered?

Good Cause covers most market-rate tenants in New York State — but with important exemptions. Units built within the last 30 years, owner-occupied buildings with 10 or fewer units, units rented for more than 245% of the area fair market rent, condominiums, and cooperative apartments are generally exempt.

✅ NYC Note

NYC tenants in rent-stabilized apartments already had strong just-cause protections under the HSTPA. The Good Cause law primarily benefits market-rate tenants outside the existing rent stabilization system — particularly upstate New York renters who previously had very few protections.

How to Check If Your Apartment Is Rent Stabilized

The best way to check: use the NYC Housing Connect portal or look up your building on the NYS HCR (Homes and Community Renewal) website. You can also check your lease — rent-stabilized leases must include a rider explaining your stabilization rights. If you're unsure, contact the NYC Rent Guidelines Board or a tenant rights organization.

🚨 14-Day Rule

New York's 14-day deposit return deadline is stricter than most states. A landlord who fails to return the deposit or provide an itemized statement within 14 days of move-out loses the right to make any deductions at all — even for legitimate damage. This is one of the strongest deposit protection rules in the country.

Landlord Entry Rights in New York

New York law requires landlords to give reasonable advance notice before entering a tenant's unit — typically interpreted as 24 hours for non-emergency situations. While New York law does not specify an exact number of hours in all statutes (unlike California), courts and tenant advocates consistently apply the 24-hour standard.

Permitted reasons for entry: repairs and inspections · showing the unit to prospective tenants or buyers · emergency situations · court-authorized purposes.

Emergency entry: Landlords may enter without notice only for genuine emergencies — fire, gas leak, burst pipe, or other immediate threats to health or safety.

Landlords who repeatedly enter without notice may be violating the tenant's right to quiet enjoyment — a separate cause of action under New York law. Document every unauthorized entry with dates and times.

Repair and Habitability Rights in New York

New York's warranty of habitability (Real Property Law § 235-b) requires landlords to maintain rental units fit for human habitation. This includes maintaining essential services such as heat, hot water, electricity, and structural safety.

Heat and Hot Water — New York City

NYC has specific heating requirements: landlords must provide heat when outdoor temperatures fall below 55°F between October 1 and May 31. During daytime hours (6am–10pm), indoor temperature must be at least 68°F. At night (10pm–6am), indoor temperature must be at least 62°F.

Hot water must be provided year-round at a minimum temperature of 120°F.

Repair Remedies in New York

If your landlord fails to make repairs, New York tenants have several options: file a complaint with NYC's 311 system (triggers inspection) · file a housing court HP proceeding to compel repairs · withhold rent pending repairs (with proper legal procedure) · repair-and-deduct (for smaller repairs in some circumstances).

Tenant Rights Outside New York City

Upstate New York tenants historically had fewer protections than NYC renters — but this changed significantly with the 2024 Good Cause Eviction Law. Here is how protections differ across the state:

🏙️ New York City

Full HSTPA protections, rent stabilization (if eligible), Good Cause for non-stabilized units, 14-day deposit return, NYC-specific heat and habitability codes.

🏘️ Buffalo & Rochester

Good Cause Eviction applies. Local habitability codes. No local rent control. Strong statewide deposit protections under HSTPA. Vacancy decontrol does not apply.

🏡 Albany & Syracuse

Good Cause Eviction applies. Statewide deposit cap (1 month). No local rent control. Warranty of habitability applies. Local housing courts handle disputes.

🌳 Westchester & Long Island

Some municipalities have local tenant protections. Good Cause applies statewide. Deposit law same as NYC. Check local ordinances for additional protections.

The main exception is if your unit is exempt from Good Cause coverage — new construction, high-rent units, small owner-occupied buildings.

It does NOT apply to: units in buildings built within the last 30 years, units renting above 245% of the local fair market rent, owner-occupied buildings with 10 or fewer units, condos, and co-ops. If you're unsure whether you're covered, contact a local tenant rights organization.

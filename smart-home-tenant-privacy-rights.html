Smart Home Devices & Your Privacy Rights as a Renter

Landlords are installing smart cameras, smart locks, video doorbells, and surveillance systems at a rapid pace. What can they legally monitor? What requires your consent? And what new 2026 laws now protect you?

Updated: July 2026 Read time: 10 minutes Coverage: Federal + All 50 States
⚡ Quick Answer

Your landlord cannot place cameras, listening devices, or surveillance equipment inside your rental unit — ever. In common areas, rules vary by state but notice and disclosure are increasingly required. Smart locks and thermostats in your unit require your consent. New 2026 state laws are expanding these protections rapidly.

What Is Happening in Rentals in 2026

The proliferation of affordable smart home technology has fundamentally changed what landlords can — and increasingly attempt to — monitor about their tenants. Video doorbells, smart locks with access logs, motion-activated cameras, connected thermostats, and even noise monitoring devices are now installed in millions of US rental units.

For tenants, this creates a new category of privacy concern that existing tenant rights law was not designed to address. The right to "quiet enjoyment" of your home — a cornerstone of landlord-tenant law — is now being tested by technology that can track your comings and goings, monitor your energy use, log who enters your home, and in some cases, listen to sounds inside your unit.

⚠️ The Core Legal Principle

No matter what technology is involved, your right to privacy inside your rental unit remains absolute. A landlord cannot use any device — smart or otherwise — to monitor, record, or surveil activity inside your home without your explicit consent. This is true in all 50 states.

Inside Your Unit vs. Common Areas — A Critical Distinction

The law draws a sharp line between surveillance inside your private rental unit and surveillance in common areas of a building. Understanding this distinction is essential.

Inside Your Rental Unit

Your landlord has no legal right to install or operate any surveillance device — camera, microphone, motion sensor, or otherwise — inside your private rental unit without your explicit written consent. This applies to:

Hidden cameras of any kind · Smart speakers or listening devices · Indoor security cameras in bedrooms, bathrooms, living areas · Motion sensors that report movement data to the landlord · Any device that monitors activity inside your home.

Placing a camera inside your unit without consent is a criminal offense in most states — not just a civil violation. It may constitute illegal wiretapping, voyeurism, or invasion of privacy depending on the circumstances.

Common Areas of the Building

Landlords generally have more latitude in common areas — hallways, lobbies, laundry rooms, parking lots, and building exteriors. However, even here, many states now require: advance written disclosure to tenants of any surveillance equipment · clear visible signage indicating cameras are present · prohibition on cameras in any area where privacy is expected (bathrooms, changing areas) · limits on how long footage can be retained.

Smart Locks — A Special Problem for Renters

Smart locks on individual unit doors present a unique privacy issue. Unlike a traditional key, a smart lock creates a detailed log of every entry and exit — who came in, when, and how long they stayed. If your landlord controls the smart lock on your unit door, they potentially have access to this intimate data about your daily life.

Key legal questions around smart locks:

Who controls the device? If you have full administrative control of the smart lock on your door — and your landlord does not have backend access to entry logs — this is less problematic. If your landlord retains admin access to your unit's lock, this raises serious privacy concerns.

Was it disclosed? Any smart lock installed in your unit should have been disclosed before you signed your lease, including what data it collects and who can access that data.

Can you change it? In most states, tenants have the right to install their own lock (as long as they provide the landlord a copy of the key). If a landlord's smart lock prevents this, consult a tenant attorney.

🔐 Your Right to Change Locks

In most states, you have the right to add or change the lock on your unit door, provided you give your landlord a copy of the new key. If you are uncomfortable with your landlord's access to a smart lock, installing your own traditional deadbolt (and providing a key) may be a legally sound solution in your state — check local law first.

What to Do If You Discover Unauthorized Surveillance

1

Document What You Found

Photograph the device, its location, and any wiring. Do not remove or tamper with the device before consulting an attorney — tampering could affect your legal case.

2

Contact Law Enforcement

Undisclosed cameras inside a rental unit may constitute criminal voyeurism or illegal wiretapping — both serious crimes. Filing a police report creates an official record and may trigger criminal investigation.

3

Send Written Notice to Your Landlord

Notify your landlord in writing of what you discovered and demand immediate removal. Their response (or lack thereof) becomes important evidence.

4

Consult a Tenant Rights Attorney Immediately

Unauthorized surveillance in a rental unit is one of the most serious violations of tenant rights. Damages can be significant. Many attorneys handle these cases on contingency.

5

Consider Breaking Your Lease

Discovery of illegal surveillance by a landlord may constitute constructive eviction — giving you the right to break your lease penalty-free. Document everything before vacating.

✅ How to Check for Hidden Cameras

RF detectors ($30-100) can detect wireless camera signals. Lens detectors use light reflection to spot camera lenses. For a free method: turn off all lights and use your phone's camera to scan the room — some IR-emitting cameras will appear as purple/white dots on your phone screen. Check common hiding spots: smoke detectors, USB chargers, wall clocks, and air purifiers.

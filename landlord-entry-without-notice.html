Can My Landlord Enter Without Notice?

Updated June 2026  ·  8 min read  ·  All 50 States Covered
⚡ Quick Answer

In almost every US state, your landlord must give advance notice — usually 24 to 48 hours — before entering your rental unit. Entering without notice (except in genuine emergencies) is illegal and may entitle you to damages.

The General Rule on Landlord Entry

Your rental unit is your home. Even though your landlord owns the property, you have a legal right to "quiet enjoyment" — meaning you have the right to live there peacefully without unexpected intrusions.

In the vast majority of US states, landlords must provide written or verbal notice at least 24 hours in advance before entering your unit. Many states require 48 hours. A few states have no specific statute but courts still expect "reasonable notice."

Common valid reasons a landlord can legally enter include:

Valid Reasons for Landlord Entry

Making repairs or inspections · Showing the unit to prospective tenants or buyers · Delivering important notices · Conducting routine inspections (with proper notice) · Emergency situations (no notice required)

What Counts as a Genuine Emergency?

Emergencies are the only exception where landlords can legally enter without any notice. But "emergency" has a specific legal meaning — it cannot be used as a loophole.

True emergencies include: burst pipes flooding the unit, fire or smoke, gas leaks, broken heating in dangerously cold weather, or any situation that poses an immediate risk to health or safety.

These are NOT emergencies: a dripping faucet, replacing a lightbulb, checking on the unit "just to see how it looks," showing the unit to a buyer without notice, or picking up mail left at the door.

🚨 Red Flag

If your landlord claims "emergency" to enter frequently without notice, this may be illegal harassment. Document every instance with dates and times.

What to Do If Your Landlord Enters Illegally

If your landlord entered your unit without proper notice, you have options. Here's what to do immediately:

1

Document Everything

Write down the exact date, time, and reason given (if any). Take photos if anything was moved or damaged. Keep a written log of every unauthorized entry.

2

Send a Written Notice to Your Landlord

Send an email or letter stating that they entered without proper notice and that you expect them to follow the law going forward. Keep a copy for yourself.

3

Contact Your Local Tenant Rights Organization

Most cities have free tenant rights organizations. They can advise you on your specific situation and even help mediate disputes.

4

File a Complaint or Take Legal Action

Depending on your state, you may be able to sue for damages, terminate your lease, or receive a rent reduction. In many states, illegal entry allows you to sue for 1-3 months rent as damages.

When Entry Becomes Harassment

A single unauthorized entry may be a mistake. But a pattern of unwanted entries — even with notice — can legally qualify as landlord harassment.

Signs that entry has crossed into harassment include: entering more than once per week without a clear maintenance reason, entering at unusual hours (very early morning or late at night), entering when the landlord knows you're not home repeatedly, or making you feel surveilled in your own home.

💡 Important

In many states, tenant harassment is a separate violation with its own penalties. If this is happening to you, consult a tenant rights attorney — many offer free initial consultations.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can my landlord enter to show my apartment to new tenants?
Yes, but they must still give proper notice (usually 24-48 hours). They cannot show your apartment at will or at unreasonable hours.

Can my landlord enter if I'm not home?
Yes, with proper notice. Your landlord does not need you to be present to enter for repairs or inspections, as long as they gave you adequate advance notice.

What if my lease says the landlord can enter any time?
Lease clauses that waive your right to notice are often unenforceable. State law typically overrides lease terms when the lease gives the landlord more rights than the law allows.

Can I change my locks to stop my landlord from entering?
This depends on your state. In many states, changing locks without providing your landlord a copy of the new key is a lease violation. Check your local laws first.

My landlord entered and stole something. What do I do?
File a police report immediately. Then document all evidence and contact a tenant rights attorney. This is both a civil matter (lease violation) and potentially a criminal one.

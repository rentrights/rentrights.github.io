Can your landlord enter your home without notice?

In almost every US state, your landlord must give 24–48 hours advance notice before entering your rental unit. Entering without notice — outside of genuine emergencies — is illegal, and entitles you to remedies.

Frequently Asked Questions

A Pay or Quit notice is the first formal step in the eviction process. Your landlord is demanding you pay overdue rent within a set period (usually 3–5 days) or vacate the property. Receiving this notice does not mean you are evicted — the landlord must still file a court case if you don't comply or leave.

No. Normal wear and tear — small nail holes, faded paint, carpet worn from regular foot traffic — cannot be deducted from your security deposit in any US state. Landlords can only charge for damage beyond normal use, such as large holes, stains, or broken fixtures caused by the tenant.

Constructive eviction occurs when a landlord makes living conditions so uninhabitable — by ignoring critical repairs — that a tenant is effectively forced to leave. In this case, the tenant may be able to legally break the lease without penalty and may sue the landlord for damages and relocation costs.

Yes. Even without a written lease, you have a verbal month-to-month tenancy protected by your state's landlord-tenant laws. Your landlord must still provide proper entry notice, maintain habitable conditions, and follow legal eviction procedures. No written lease does not mean no rights.

No. Under the federal Fair Housing Act, landlords cannot discriminate based on familial status, which includes families with children under 18. Exceptions exist only for certain qualified senior housing communities. Refusing to rent to a family with children is illegal and can result in significant civil penalties.

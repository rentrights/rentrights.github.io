Landlord Harassment — What Is Illegal & How to Stop It

Landlord harassment is more than just annoying — in most states it is illegal and actionable. Here is exactly what qualifies, how to document it, and how to make it stop using the law.

Updated: July 2026 Read time: 10 minutes Coverage: All 50 States
⚡ Quick Answer

Landlord harassment — repeated unwanted contact, threats, illegal entry, removing services, or intimidation to force you out — is illegal in most US states. Tenants who experience it can seek damages, injunctions, and sometimes punitive awards. The key is thorough documentation from the very first incident.

What Is Landlord Harassment?

Landlord harassment refers to a pattern of behavior by a landlord designed to intimidate, pressure, or force a tenant to vacate their home — or to punish a tenant for exercising their legal rights. Unlike a single isolated incident, harassment typically involves repeated or sustained conduct that interferes with a tenant's right to quiet enjoyment of their home.

Courts and housing authorities look at the pattern and intent of behavior, not just individual incidents. A landlord entering your apartment once without notice may be a technical violation. A landlord entering six times in two months is a pattern that courts recognize as harassment.

⚠️ Key Legal Standard

Most harassment laws require showing a pattern of conduct — not a single incident. Document every occurrence with dates, times, and what happened. Your documented pattern is your case.

Common Examples of Illegal Landlord Harassment

🚪 Repeated Unauthorized Entry

Entering your unit without proper notice multiple times — even with a key — violates your right to quiet enjoyment and constitutes harassment when part of a pattern.

💡 Cutting Off Utilities

Shutting off heat, water, electricity, or other essential services to pressure you to leave is illegal in all 50 states — and in most states is a criminal offense.

🔒 Changing Locks

Locking you out of your own home without a court order is "self-help eviction" — illegal everywhere. You have the right to regain entry immediately.

📱 Excessive Contact

Calling, texting, or appearing at your door repeatedly at all hours without a legitimate reason — especially after being asked to stop — can constitute harassment.

🗑️ Removing Appliances

Taking away appliances, furniture, or fixtures that were part of your rental agreement to make the unit uninhabitable or pressure you to leave.

😤 Verbal Threats

Threatening illegal eviction, threatening to call immigration authorities, threatening physical harm, or using intimidating language to pressure you to vacate.

🏚️ Ignoring Repairs

Deliberately failing to make repairs to make the unit so uncomfortable that you leave voluntarily — especially when repairs were previously made promptly.

🎭 Staging "Inspections"

Scheduling repeated, unnecessary inspections as a pretext to monitor you, intimidate you, or find minor lease violations to use as grounds for eviction.

Illegal Self-Help Eviction — The Most Serious Form

The most extreme form of landlord harassment is "self-help eviction" — when a landlord takes direct action to physically remove a tenant without going through the courts. This is illegal in all 50 US states and can result in significant civil and even criminal liability for the landlord.

Illegal self-help eviction includes: changing the locks while you are away · removing your belongings from the unit · removing doors, windows, or appliances to make the unit uninhabitable · shutting off utilities · physically threatening or intimidating you to force you to leave.

🚨 If This Happens to You

If your landlord changes your locks or removes your belongings, call the police immediately. This is potentially a criminal matter in most states. You also have the right to sue for damages — which in many states includes your actual damages plus a penalty of 1-3 months rent, attorney fees, and sometimes punitive damages.

Your Legal Options

1

Send a Formal Written Warning

Put your landlord on notice in writing that their behavior is illegal and must stop immediately. Reference specific incidents with dates. Send via email AND certified mail. This often stops harassment and creates a paper trail if it doesn't.

2

File a Complaint With Housing Authorities

Contact your local housing authority, code enforcement, or rent board. Many cities have dedicated tenant protection units for harassment complaints. An official complaint creates a public record and may trigger inspections or investigations.

3

Seek a Restraining Order or Injunction

Courts can issue injunctions ordering your landlord to stop specific behaviors. For serious harassment — especially threats — a restraining order may be appropriate. This requires going to court but can provide immediate legal protection.

4

Sue for Damages

In most states, tenants can sue for actual damages, emotional distress, attorney fees, and sometimes punitive damages. Cities like New York and Los Angeles have specific harassment ordinances with substantial statutory penalties — up to $10,000 per violation in some jurisdictions.

5

Break Your Lease Penalty-Free

If harassment has made your home effectively uninhabitable or untenable, you may have grounds for constructive eviction — allowing you to break your lease without penalty and potentially recover relocation costs and damages.

Writing an Effective Demand Letter

A well-written demand letter often stops harassment without litigation. Here is what to include:

Opening: State clearly that you are a tenant at the property and that you are writing to address specific illegal conduct.

Specific incidents: List every incident with exact dates, times, and what occurred. Be factual, not emotional.

Legal citations: Reference your state's landlord harassment law or quiet enjoyment statute by name if possible.

Demand: State clearly what you demand — that the specific behavior stops immediately.

Consequence: State that if the behavior continues, you will pursue all available legal remedies including filing complaints with housing authorities, seeking an injunction, and suing for damages.

Deadline: Give a specific deadline for acknowledgment — typically 7 days.

✅ Template Opening

"I am writing to formally notify you that your conduct toward me as a tenant at [address] constitutes illegal harassment under [your state's] landlord-tenant law. The following specific incidents have occurred: [list with dates]. This behavior must cease immediately. If it continues, I will pursue all available legal remedies without further notice."

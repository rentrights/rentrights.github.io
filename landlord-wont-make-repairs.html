Repairs & Habitability

Landlord Won't Make Repairs — What You Can Do

Updated June 2026  ·  10 min read  ·  All US States
⚡ Quick Answer

Your landlord is legally required to maintain a habitable living space in all 50 states. If they refuse to make essential repairs, you have several powerful legal options — including withholding rent, repairing and deducting, reporting to authorities, or even breaking your lease penalty-free.

⚖️ Not Legal Advice: Laws on repair remedies vary significantly by state. Some options (like rent withholding) require strict legal procedures. Consult a tenant attorney before taking action.

The Implied Warranty of Habitability

In every US state, landlords have a legal duty called the "implied warranty of habitability." This means that regardless of what your lease says, your landlord must maintain your rental unit in a condition fit for human habitation.

This warranty cannot be waived — even if you signed a lease saying you accept the property "as is," your landlord still has a legal obligation to keep it habitable.

✅ Key Point

You don't need anything in writing in your lease about repairs. The warranty of habitability is automatically part of every rental agreement in the US by law.

What Repairs Are Legally Required

Landlords must repair and maintain anything that affects the health, safety, or basic livability of the unit. This includes:

  • Functioning heat, especially in winter (most states require minimum 68°F)
  • Hot and cold running water and working plumbing
  • Waterproof roof, walls, and windows — no leaks
  • Working electrical systems and safe wiring
  • Pest and rodent control (if infestation existed before move-in)
  • Functioning locks on all doors and windows
  • Safe common areas — no broken stairs, railings, or lighting
  • Working smoke and carbon monoxide detectors
  • Removal of lead paint hazards (in older buildings)
  • Working garbage disposal facilities

⚠️ Important Distinction

Landlords are responsible for repairs that affect habitability. Cosmetic issues — a broken cabinet door, a cracked tile, worn paint — may not rise to the level of habitability violations, though they may still violate your lease terms.

Steps to Take Before Using Legal Remedies

Before taking any legal action, you must follow a proper process. Skipping steps can weaken your case or expose you to liability.

1

Document the Problem Thoroughly

Take dated photos and videos of every issue. Note when the problem started. This evidence is critical if you end up in court.

2

Submit a Written Repair Request

Always put repair requests in writing — email is fine. Describe the problem clearly and request a specific repair deadline (e.g., "within 14 days"). Keep copies of everything.

3

Give Your Landlord Reasonable Time to Respond

What is "reasonable" depends on urgency. A broken heater in winter = 24–48 hours. A non-urgent repair = 2–4 weeks. Courts expect you to give fair notice before escalating.

4

Send a Follow-Up Notice

If your landlord ignores you, send a second written notice stating that you will take legal action if the repair is not made within a specific deadline. This creates a paper trail.

5

Now Use One of Your Legal Options Below

Only after following steps 1–4 should you pursue formal remedies. Acting too quickly without documentation can backfire.

Your 5 Legal Options

Option 1 — Most Common

Report to Local Housing Authority

File a complaint with your city or county housing code enforcement office. An inspector will visit and cite your landlord for violations. This creates official documentation and puts legal pressure on your landlord to act.

Available in all states. Free to file. No risk to your tenancy if done properly.

Option 2 — Powerful but Risky

Repair and Deduct

In about 30 states, you can hire someone to make essential repairs yourself and deduct the cost from your next rent payment — up to a legal limit (usually 1 month's rent). You must follow strict procedures: written notice, landlord failure to act, receipts for work done.

Available in: CA, TX, AZ, WA, OR, MT, ND, SD, KS, MO, MI, OH, VA, SC, and others. Check your state law first.

Option 3 — High Risk, High Reward

Rent Withholding / Rent Escrow

In some states, you can withhold rent or pay it into a court escrow account until repairs are made. This is a powerful tool but requires strict legal compliance — if done wrong, it can result in eviction. Never withhold rent without legal advice first.

Available in: MA, NY, NJ, MD, PA, HI, and others. Requires specific procedures — consult an attorney.

Option 4 — Escape the Lease

Constructive Eviction / Break Your Lease

If conditions are so bad that the unit is effectively uninhabitable, you may be able to legally break your lease without penalty. This is called "constructive eviction." You typically must vacate the unit to claim this — and must have documented the conditions thoroughly first.

Recognized in most states. Courts look for: documented conditions, written notice given, landlord failure to act, and tenant vacating promptly.

Option 5 — Sue for Damages

Take Your Landlord to Court

You can sue your landlord for: rent reduction (for time you paid full rent in uninhabitable conditions), out-of-pocket costs, moving costs if you had to relocate, and emotional distress in serious cases. Small claims court handles most of these without a lawyer needed.

Available in all states. Small claims limits range from $2,500 to $25,000 depending on state.

Protect Yourself from Retaliation

Many tenants fear that requesting repairs or reporting violations will lead to retaliation — rent increases, eviction notices, or harassment. Retaliatory actions by landlords are illegal in most states.

If you report a code violation or exercise your legal rights, and your landlord retaliates within a short period (usually 60–180 days), most states create a legal presumption that the action was retaliatory — shifting the burden of proof to your landlord.

🛡️ How to Protect Yourself

Keep records of all repair requests with dates. Document any retaliatory actions (rent increase notices, eviction filings) with dates relative to your complaint. This timeline is your protection in court.

Frequently Asked Questions

My heat is broken in winter. How fast must my landlord respond?
Most states require emergency repairs affecting health and safety — like heat in cold weather — to be addressed within 24–72 hours. If your landlord won't act, contact your local housing authority immediately.

Can I move to a hotel and make my landlord pay?
In cases of emergency uninhabitable conditions (like a gas leak or flood), some states allow you to deduct temporary housing costs from rent. Document everything and check your state's rules.

My landlord says the repair is my responsibility. Is that true?
It depends on what it is and what your lease says. Tenants are typically responsible for minor issues caused by their own use. Landlords are responsible for anything affecting habitability and structural elements.

Can I stop paying rent until repairs are made?
Only in states with rent withholding laws, and only after following specific legal procedures. Never just stop paying without legal advice — this can lead to eviction even if your landlord is in the wrong.

What if my landlord makes partial repairs but ignores the main problem?
Document what was and wasn't fixed. Send another written notice about remaining issues. Partial compliance does not release a landlord from their full legal obligation.

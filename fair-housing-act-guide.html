The Fair Housing Act — Your Right to Be Free from Discrimination

Federal law makes it illegal for landlords to discriminate against tenants based on race, religion, sex, disability, and more. Here is everything you need to know — and what to do if your rights are violated.

Updated: June 2026Read time: 10 minutesCoverage: Federal Law · All 50 States
⚡ Quick Answer

The Fair Housing Act prohibits landlords from refusing to rent, setting different terms, or treating tenants differently based on race, color, religion, national origin, sex, disability, or familial status. Violators face fines up to $21,039 for a first offense — and much more for repeat violations.

What Is the Fair Housing Act?

The Fair Housing Act (FHA) is a federal civil rights law enacted in 1968 and significantly strengthened in 1988. It prohibits discrimination in the sale, rental, and financing of housing based on specific protected characteristics. The law applies to virtually all residential housing in the United States.

The FHA is enforced by the US Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) and can also be enforced through private lawsuits in federal court. Many states and cities have their own fair housing laws that add additional protected classes beyond the federal minimum.

✅ Who Is Covered

The Fair Housing Act applies to landlords, property managers, real estate agents, lenders, and anyone else involved in the rental or sale of most residential housing. Very limited exceptions exist for owner-occupied buildings with four or fewer units and certain religious organizations.

The 7 Federally Protected Classes

✊

Race & Color

Discrimination based on race or skin color in any aspect of housing

🛐

Religion

Refusing to rent or treating differently based on religious beliefs

🌍

National Origin

Discrimination based on country of birth or ancestry

⚥

Sex

Includes sexual harassment and gender-based discrimination

♿

Disability

Physical or mental disability; includes reasonable accommodation rights

👨‍👩‍👧

Familial Status

Families with children under 18; pregnant women; legal guardians

Many states add additional protected classes including source of income, sexual orientation, gender identity, age, marital status, and veteran status. Check your state's fair housing laws for the full list in your area.

What Is Illegal Under the Fair Housing Act

Legal Landlord Actions ✓
Screening all applicants using the same objective criteria
Rejecting applicants based on poor credit or rental history
Enforcing the same lease terms for all tenants
Requiring the same income verification from everyone
Restricting smoking in all units equally
Illegal Discrimination ✗
Refusing to rent to someone because of their race or religion
Telling a minority applicant a unit is unavailable when it is not
Charging higher rent or deposits based on protected class
Refusing to make reasonable accommodations for a disability
Refusing to rent to families with children (unless senior housing)

Reasonable Accommodations for Disability

The Fair Housing Act requires landlords to make reasonable accommodations for tenants with disabilities. This means changing rules, policies, or practices when necessary to give a person with a disability an equal opportunity to use and enjoy their home.

Examples include: allowing a service or emotional support animal in a no-pets building · providing a reserved accessible parking space · allowing a tenant to make accessibility modifications to the unit (at their own expense) · accepting rent via mail if a disability prevents in-person payment.

How to Recognize Housing Discrimination

Discrimination is often subtle and difficult to detect. Here are signs that you may be experiencing illegal housing discrimination:

You were told a unit was unavailable — but you saw it listed as available shortly after, or a friend of a different background was offered the unit.

You were quoted a higher price than advertised, or terms different from those offered to other similarly qualified applicants.

The landlord asked illegal questions — such as your country of origin, whether you have children, your religion, or your disability status.

Your reasonable accommodation request was denied without a legitimate business justification — especially for service animals or accessibility modifications.

🔍 Testing for Discrimination

Fair housing organizations sometimes use "testers" — people of different backgrounds who inquire about the same unit to document differential treatment. If you suspect discrimination, contact your local fair housing organization — they may be able to conduct a test on your behalf.

How to File a Fair Housing Complaint

1

Document Everything Immediately

Write down everything that happened — dates, times, exact words spoken, names of witnesses. Save all emails, texts, and written correspondence. Evidence becomes harder to gather as time passes.

2

File with HUD

Submit a complaint with the US Department of Housing and Urban Development at hud.gov/fairhousing. You have one year from the discriminatory act to file. The complaint is free. HUD will investigate at no cost to you.

3

Contact Your State or Local Fair Housing Agency

Many states and cities have their own fair housing agencies with additional protections and faster response times than the federal process. Search "[your city/state] fair housing complaint" to find the right agency.

4

Consult a Fair Housing Attorney

You can also file a private lawsuit in federal court. Many fair housing attorneys work on contingency — no fee unless you win. Damages can include actual damages, emotional distress, and punitive damages up to $50,000 for first offenses.

💡 Penalties for Violations

First-time FHA violations: civil penalties up to $21,039. Second violation within 5 years: up to $52,596. Additional violations within 7 years: up to $105,194. Private lawsuit damages can be significantly higher, including punitive damages and attorney fees.

Normal Wear and Tear vs. Damage

Updated June 2026  ·  9 min read  ·  Room-by-Room Examples
⚡ Quick Answer

Normal wear and tear is the gradual deterioration that happens from everyday living — landlords cannot charge you for it. Damage is harm caused by negligence, misuse, or accidents — landlords can deduct for this. The line between the two is often disputed, but real examples make it clear.

⚖️ Not Legal Advice: This guide provides general educational information only. What qualifies as wear and tear vs. damage can vary by state. Consult a local attorney for your specific situation.

The Legal Definition of Normal Wear and Tear

Courts across the US define normal wear and tear as deterioration that occurs through ordinary, reasonable use of the property over time — without negligence, carelessness, misuse, or abuse by the tenant.

The key factors courts consider are: how long the tenant lived there (longer tenancy = more expected wear), the age of the item (old carpet vs. new), and whether the use was reasonable for the number of occupants.

✅ The Golden Rule

If the deterioration would have happened to any reasonable tenant living there for the same amount of time — it is wear and tear. If it happened because of something the tenant did wrong — it is damage.

Useful Life of Common Items

Landlords must account for the remaining useful life of an item when charging for damage. If carpet had a 10-year life and was already 8 years old when you moved in, they cannot charge you full replacement cost — only the remaining 20% value.

ItemAverage Useful Life
Carpet8–10 years
Interior paint3–5 years
Hardwood floors25+ years
Appliances (fridge, stove)10–15 years
Blinds / window coverings5–7 years
Bathroom fixtures10–15 years
Countertops (laminate)10–15 years
Countertops (granite/quartz)20–30 years
Cabinet hardware10+ years

💡 How to Use This

If your landlord charges you $800 to replace carpet that was already 9 years old (with a 10-year life expectancy), you should only owe 10% of the cost — about $80. The landlord cannot charge you for replacing something that was nearly at the end of its life anyway.

How to Dispute Unfair Deductions

Step 1 — Compare move-in and move-out photos. This is why documenting your unit on day one is so important. Side-by-side photos are the most powerful evidence in deposit disputes.

Step 2 — Request itemized receipts. In most states, landlords must provide receipts for all deductions. If they can't prove the cost, the deduction may be invalid.

Step 3 — Send a written dispute letter. Write a calm, factual letter citing which deductions you dispute and why. Send it via email so you have a record.

Step 4 — File in small claims court. Most security deposit disputes are resolved in small claims court. No lawyer required. In many states, wrongful withholding means you get 2–3x the disputed amount back.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can a landlord charge me for carpet cleaning every time I move out?
Not automatically. If you left the carpet reasonably clean, routine end-of-tenancy cleaning is considered wear and tear. They can only charge for cleaning if the carpet was left significantly dirtier than normal.

Can a landlord charge me for repainting the entire apartment?
Only if you caused damage beyond normal wear — unauthorized paint colors, large marks, or stains on walls. Standard repainting after a normal tenancy is a landlord's responsibility.

What if I lived there for 5 years and the carpet is destroyed from use?
Heavy wear after 5 years of normal use is likely wear and tear, not damage. Courts expect more deterioration the longer a tenant lives somewhere.

My landlord says I owe for "general cleaning." Is that valid?
Only if you left the unit significantly dirty. Routine cleaning after move-out is typically the landlord's responsibility unless the unit was left in unusually poor condition.

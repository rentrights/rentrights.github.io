Renters Insurance — Complete Guide for 2026

Most renters don't have it. Most wish they did after a fire, theft, or water damage. Here is everything you need to know about renters insurance — what it covers, what it costs, and whether you actually need it.

Updated: July 2026 Read time: 11 minutes Coverage: All US States
⚡ Quick Answer

Renters insurance costs $10–$25 per month and covers your personal belongings against theft, fire, and water damage — plus liability if someone is injured in your home. Your landlord's insurance covers the building only — not your possessions. It is one of the best financial decisions any renter can make.

Why Every Renter Needs This

The most common misconception in renting: "My landlord has insurance, so I'm covered." This is completely false. Your landlord's insurance covers the physical building — walls, roof, plumbing, electrical. It does not cover a single item you own.

If a fire destroys your apartment, your landlord's insurance will pay to rebuild the walls. Your laptop, furniture, clothing, jewelry, and every other possession you own? Gone — unless you have renters insurance. At an average cost of $15/month, renters insurance is one of the best financial decisions a renter can make.

💡 Real Example

A burst pipe floods your apartment and destroys your furniture, electronics, and clothing worth $15,000. Without renters insurance: you pay everything out of pocket. With renters insurance: you file a claim and receive a check after your deductible. The policy costs $15/month = $180/year. One claim can save you thousands.

Loss of Use / Additional Living Expenses

One of the most valuable and overlooked parts of renters insurance is loss of use coverage. If your apartment becomes uninhabitable due to a covered event (fire, major water damage), your policy pays for temporary housing — hotel, short-term rental — until you can return or find a new place. This can easily be worth thousands of dollars in a single claim.

Liability Coverage

If a guest slips and falls in your apartment and sues you, your renters insurance liability coverage pays for your legal defense and any settlement or judgment — up to your policy limit (typically $100,000). Without this, a single lawsuit could be financially devastating.

Actual Cash Value vs. Replacement Cost — Critical Difference

This is the most important decision when choosing a renters insurance policy — and most people don't understand it until they file a claim.

Actual Cash Value (ACV) — pays you what your belongings were worth at the time of loss, accounting for depreciation. Your 3-year-old $1,200 laptop might only be valued at $400. Your 5-year-old $2,000 sofa might be worth $600. This type of coverage is cheaper but pays less.

Replacement Cost Value (RCV) — pays what it actually costs to replace the item with a new equivalent today, regardless of age. Your 3-year-old laptop? You get a comparable new laptop. This coverage costs 10–15% more but is significantly more valuable when you actually file a claim.

⚠️ Our Strong Recommendation

Always choose Replacement Cost Value coverage. The premium difference is typically just $2–5/month, but the claim payout difference can be thousands of dollars. The point of insurance is to make you whole — ACV policies often leave you far short of that goal.

Can Your Landlord Require Renters Insurance?

Yes — in most US states, landlords can legally require tenants to carry renters insurance as a condition of the lease. This requirement must be stated in your lease agreement. A landlord cannot retroactively require insurance after your lease is signed without your agreement.

If your lease requires renters insurance, you must maintain it throughout your tenancy and may be required to provide proof of coverage (a certificate of insurance naming your landlord as an interested party). Failing to maintain required insurance may be a lease violation that could trigger eviction proceedings in some states.

✅ Even If Not Required

Even if your landlord doesn't require it, carrying renters insurance is strongly advisable. The average renter has $20,000–$30,000 in personal belongings. Losing everything in a fire without insurance is financially devastating — and completely preventable for $15/month.

How to Get Renters Insurance — Step by Step

1

Inventory Your Belongings

Walk through your home and estimate the value of everything you own — furniture, electronics, clothing, kitchen items, jewelry. This determines how much personal property coverage you need. Most renters underestimate this: $20,000–$35,000 is typical.

2

Choose Your Coverage Amount

Select personal property coverage that matches your inventory. Choose liability coverage of at least $100,000 — $300,000 is better and costs very little extra. Select Replacement Cost Value, not Actual Cash Value.

3

Get Multiple Quotes

Compare at least 3 providers. Major options include Lemonade (app-based, fast claims), State Farm, Allstate, GEICO, Progressive, and Nationwide. If you have auto insurance, get a bundling quote from your current provider first.

4

Review Policy Details Carefully

Before buying, confirm: coverage type (RCV vs ACV), deductible amount, specific exclusions, loss of use coverage amount, and liability limits. Read the actual policy document, not just the marketing summary.

5

Document Your Belongings

After getting covered, create a home inventory — photos or video of all your possessions. Store this in the cloud. If you ever need to file a claim, this documentation makes the process much smoother and faster.

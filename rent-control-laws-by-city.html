Rent Control Laws by City 2026

Rent control and rent stabilization laws limit how much your landlord can raise your rent each year. Here is what the law actually says in America's biggest cities — and how to find out if you're protected.

Updated: July 2026 Read time: 12 minutes Coverage: 20+ Major US Cities
⚡ Quick Answer

Rent control exists in fewer than 200 US cities — mostly in California, New York, New Jersey, Oregon, and Washington DC. If you live in a rent-controlled unit, your landlord can only raise rent by a set percentage each year (often 3–5%). Most US renters are NOT covered — check your city below.

What Is Rent Control — And Who Does It Cover?

Rent control (also called rent stabilization) is a local or state law that limits how much a landlord can increase rent each year. The goal is to keep housing affordable and prevent displacement of long-term tenants in high-cost markets.

There are two main types: Strict rent control freezes rent at a fixed amount, with increases only allowed under specific circumstances. Rent stabilization (more common) allows annual increases up to a set percentage, often tied to inflation or a local rent board's decision.

⚠️ Critical Point

Even in cities with rent control, not every unit is covered. Most rent control laws exempt: units built after a certain year (new construction), single-family homes, condos, and luxury units above a certain rent threshold. Check your specific unit's status with your local rent board.

New York City has the largest rent stabilization system in the country. The Rent Guidelines Board sets allowable increases each year. For 2026, the board approved a 2.75% increase for one-year leases and 5.25% for two-year leases.

Who is covered: Buildings with 6+ units built before 1974, with monthly rent under the deregulation threshold (currently $305,000/year). The 2019 Housing Stability and Tenant Protection Act significantly strengthened these protections.

  • Landlords cannot raise rent above the allowable guideline
  • Tenants have the right to lease renewal
  • Succession rights allow family members to inherit rent-stabilized leases
  • Check your status: apps.hcr.ny.gov

Los Angeles's Rent Stabilization Ordinance covers apartments built on or before October 1, 1978. The annual allowable increase is the lower of 3% or the regional Consumer Price Index (CPI). In 2026, CPI is below 3%, so the effective limit is the CPI figure.

Just Cause Eviction: LA also has strong just-cause eviction protections — landlords must have a legally recognized reason to evict RSO tenants, and must pay relocation assistance for no-fault evictions.

  • Check if your unit is covered: zimas.lacity.org
  • File complaints: LA Housing Department (LAHD)
  • Relocation assistance: 1–3 months rent for no-fault evictions
  • State law (AB 1482) also covers many units built after 1978

San Francisco's Rent Ordinance covers most residential buildings with 2+ units built before June 13, 1979. The annual allowable increase is set at 60% of the regional CPI. For the period beginning March 1, 2026, the allowable increase is 1.7%.

San Francisco also has one of the strongest just-cause eviction laws in the country — landlords must cite one of 16 specific just-cause reasons to evict, and many no-fault evictions require substantial relocation payments.

  • Check your unit: sfrent.org
  • Landlords must serve a notice of allowable increase
  • Tenants can petition for reductions in services
  • Ellis Act evictions (removing units from market) require large relocation payments

Washington D.C. has a comprehensive rent control law covering most rental units. Landlords can increase rent once per year by the lesser of CPI + 2% or 10%. For elderly tenants (62+) or tenants with disabilities, increases are limited to CPI only.

  • Exemptions: buildings with 4 or fewer units where owner lives in one, buildings built after 1975
  • Landlords must register units with the Rent Administrator
  • Tenants can challenge improper increases at the Office of Administrative Hearings
  • Check status: dcra.dc.gov

Chicago has no rent control. Illinois state law actually preempts local governments from enacting rent control ordinances. However, Chicago does have the Residential Landlord and Tenant Ordinance (RLTO), one of the strongest local tenant protection laws in the country.

While landlords can raise rent by any amount, they must provide 60 days written notice for increases over 10% and must follow strict rules on security deposits, repairs, and lease renewals.

  • No cap on rent increases
  • 60 days notice required for increases above 10%
  • Strong security deposit interest requirements
  • Strict habitability and repair standards

States Where Rent Control Is Banned

Many US states have passed laws preempting local governments from enacting rent control. If you live in these states, no city or county can legally implement rent control regardless of local desire:

Alabama · Arizona · Florida · Georgia · Idaho · Illinois · Indiana · Iowa · Kansas · Kentucky · Louisiana · Michigan · Missouri · Montana · Nebraska · Nevada (with exceptions) · North Carolina · Ohio · Oklahoma · Pennsylvania · South Carolina · Tennessee · Texas · Virginia · Wisconsin · Wyoming

✅ Even Without Rent Control

Even if your city has no rent control, landlords still must give proper written notice before raising rent (usually 30–60 days), cannot raise rent mid-lease, and cannot retaliate against you for exercising legal rights. See our full guide on rent increase rights for more.
